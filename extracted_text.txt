Abstract—The legal documents issued by the Government of India 
tend to be formal, lengthy, opaque, and supremely difficult for the 
ordinary citizen to digest. While parsing this legalese is a daunting task 
for any one person, we take the position that a citizen ca nnot have 
enough cognitive ability to extract accurate meaning and therefore 
take action on their own behalf or for their community. This paper 
describes an AI-driven LegalTech framework called CodeBandhu that 
takes any Indian legal or policy document, and simplifies, summarizes, 
and suggests actions therefrom surfaces as recommend actions for 
users. 
The CodeBandhu system draws upon NLP (Natural Language 
Processing) techniques for unsupervised text simplification, 
abstractive and extractive summarization, and argument -mining to 
automatically take heirarchical illegal dense material, and present in 
simplified and nuggeted form. 
The model’s modular pipeline consists of document normalisation, 
lexical simplification using Legal -BERT for basic simplification, 
rhetorical role annotations, and a recommender engine based on 
legal issues and back precedents recognized. CodeBandhu is tra ined 
and evaluated against publicly available datasets, including notably 
LawSum and IN -Abs (Indian Abstractive Legal Summaries), and 
validation along both automatic metrics (ROUGE, BLEU, F1-Intent) as 
well as generated for experts and then validated by hu man experts. 
The results demonstrate an enormously increase in readability and 
relevance of the summaries, and an alignment with legal practitioners 
preferences. The aim of CodeBandhu is to assist scraping and 
simplifying legal, policy the materials in a c itizen friendly format and 
suggesting feasible actions to promote greater legal literacy, as well as 
faster compreh veering to and civic acting on corresponding policy.

I. INTRODUCTION 
The ability to access legal information is a primary condition 
for individuals to exercise their civil rights, yet this access is 
restricted not by the absence of laws, but by the opacity of 
their meaning and design in many municipalities around the 
world, including India. Government produced documentation, 
especially legal materials including policies, judicial decisions, 
laws enacted by Parliament, and responses to a Right to 
Information request, are often crafted in complicated legal 
shorthand with arcan e terminologies and procedural 
conventions that are without any meaning for a lay reader. 
Without any judgement on the worth or merit of such 
documents for members of the general public, interpreting 
them to allow individuals to make informed decisions or take 
legal action is not only impractical, it is arguably impossible 
without expensive legal support. 
As India’s digital governance agenda expands with platforms 
such as Digital India, e -Courts, IndiaCode, and others, citizens 
will be afforded greater access to legal texts. Access to legal 
texts does not imply comprehension, however. Legal 
documents are us ually designed for use by courts and 
practitioners of law, not for public comprehension. 
Furthermore, many government documents lack a structured 
presentation of meaning, and are presented in unstructured or 
scanned documents, which may also lead to proble matic 
automatic parsing and understanding. 
1.1 The Problem 
Today, India’s judicial system faces an unprecedented 
backlog of over 4.7 crore pending cases at various levels of 
courts. Apart from the sheer number of cases, it should be 
noted that the existence of such huge backlog is indicative 
of a legal system that  is under considerable stress and in 
desperate need of efficiency. One way to alleviate traffic 
jams in our legal system is to allow citizens to understand 
and navigate their legal documents on their own, without 
waiting for assistance from a trained legal  professional 
(something that is becoming increasingly difficult to do in 
India). The challenges to doing this are due to: 
• The vast majority of legal documents are long and 
syntactically complicated. 
• Legal documents have little standardization of document 
structure, particularly within the Indian context. 
• The verbiage of legal documents is heavily based on legal 
jargon and Latin. 
• There is no objective person providing legal advice about 
what a person can do based on a legal document. 
Apart from the fact that Natural Language Processing (NLP) 
has made tremendous progress in some areas such as text 
summarization, question answering, and document 
classification, its application to real legal text (i.e. to Indian 
legal documents) is still unexpectedly limited. Interestingly, 
most of the current developments in models that apply applied 
NLP to legal systems have been primarily trained with Western 
legal systems, which are comparatively different in their overall 
structure, meaning and language. 
1.2 The Solution 
Introducing CodeBandhu: In response to these challenges, we 
propose CodeBandhu, a comprehensive AI -based framework 
that acts as a companion for understanding legal codes . It is 
designed to: 
• Simplify complex legal texts by rewriting them in 
accessible plain language. 
• Summarize lengthy legal or policy documents to 
provide a concise overview. 
• Suggest user -centric, non -binding legal actions or 
solutions based on the detected intent and content 
of the document. 
The term Bandhu (meaning companion in Sanskrit) reflects the 
intent of the system: to stand by the user as an accessible legal 
aide. CodeBandhu aims to bridge the legal knowledge divide 
through advanced Natural Language Processing, Legal 
Information Retrieval, and Argumen t Mining techniques 
tailored for the Indian context Natural Language Processing , 
Legal Information Retrieval and Argument Mining . 
1.3 Technical Foundation 
Based on recent advances in legal NLP, CodeBandhu can be 
considered a combination of: 
• Text Normalization techniques are used to improve the 
general performance of summarization models such as 
BART and PEGASUS in legal texts. 
• LawSum is a weakly supervised framework that produces 
data sets tagged with rhetorical roles to select sentences 
for better summarization. 
• Legal document simplification by USLT provides a domain-
adapted approach to rewriting complex sentences with 
Legal-BERT. 
• Argument-aware summarization helps capture the logical 
structure of judgments (Issues –Reasons–Conclusions) in 
ArgLegalSumm. 
• Intent-based evaluation frameworks provide a more 
meaningful way to assess the quality of legal summaries, 
especially in domains like land, crime, or public policy. 
1.4 Research Objectives 
In this article, we concentrate on the design, development, 
and evaluation of CodeBandhu. Our primary objectives are 
to: 
• Design an end -to-end pipeline for legal document 
simplification, summarization, and advisory. 
• Adapt state-of-the-art transformer models to the Indian 
legal context. 
• Evaluate the performance of CodeBandhu on Indian legal 
data sets using automated and human-centered metrics. 
• Highlight possible advancement in legal literacy and 
better access to justice. 
1.5 Key Contributions 
The major contributions of this paper can be listed as 
follows: 
• The first known system combining legal text 
simplification, summarization, and recommendation in 
the Indian legal context. 
• A modular pipeline combining Legal -BERT, rhetorical role 
labeling, and argument mining, enriched with a 
recommendation engine to help the user choose the next 
plausible step. 
• We evaluate CodeBandhu using various scores, such as 
ROUGE, BLEU, Flesch -Kincaid Readability, and the novel 
Intent-F1 score. 
• An illustration of how such a system could improve 
comprehension and actionable insight toward Indian 
legal documents.

II. RELATED WORK 
The intricacy, length, and significance of legal texts in actual 
decision-making have made legal document processing a 
crucial field within Natural Language Processing (NLP). The four 
main areas of prior research in this field have been 
summarization, text simplification, rhetorical role analysis, and 
legal document evaluation. Few systems, particularly in India, 
have made an effort to combine all of these elements in order 
to produce legal advice outputs that are focused on the needs 
of the public. The limitations addressed by the proposed 
CodeBandhu system are highlighted in this section, which also 
reviews the most pertinent literature in each of these 
subdomains. 
A. 2.1 Legal Document Summarization 
The task of automatically summarizing legal documents is 
crucial, particularly in common law systems where precedents 
and lengthy judgments are common. Extractive summarization 
was the original application of conventional models like 
LexRank and TextRank. However, models like BART, PEGASUS, 
and Longformer have gained popularity for abstractive 
summarization since the introduction of transformers. 
Using both automated metrics and input from legal 
professionals, Shukla et al. (2022) carried out a thorough 
assessment of extractive and abstractive summarization 
models on legal texts. They pointed out a significant issue: 
input token limits prevent the majority of transformer -based 
summarizers from handling lengthy legal documents. They 
suggested a hybrid strategy that blends abstractive generation 
and extractive preprocessing to get around this. 
Similarly, to adapt domain -agnostic models like BART and 
PEGASUS for Indian legal texts —which are frequently noisy, 
scanned, or irregular in structure —Ghosh et al. (2022) 
proposed a text normalization technique. According to their 
research, normalized input greatly enhances summary quality, 
which is why we use it in CodeBandhu’s pre -processing 
pipeline. 
2.2 Simplifying Legal Text 
Complex sentence structures, outdated terminology, and 
domain-specific jargon are characteristics of legal documents. 
Text simplification techniques have been investigated to 
improve accessibility. Particularly in legal domains where 
datasets for simplific ation model training are scarce, 
traditional rule-based approaches frequently fall short in terms 
of generalization. 
USLT, an unsupervised system for simplifying legal texts, was 
presented by Cemri et al. in 2022. While recursively dividing 
lengthy sentences, USLT employs Legal-BERT to recognize and 
substitute complex words. The model maintains semantic 
integrity while g reatly enhancing readability. This method 
served as the inspiration for CodeBandhu’s simplification 
module, which uses Legal -BERT to restructure sentences and 
replace vocabulary. 
2.3 Rhetorical Role Labeling and Argument Mining 
Issues, Facts, Reasons, Precedents, and Conclusions make up 
the rhetorical framework of legal rulings. Determining and 
classifying these roles facilitates decision -making and 
summarization. 
The first extensive, rhetorical -role annotated Indian legal 
corpus is the LawSum dataset, which was first presented by 
Parikh et al. in 2021. It includes more than 10,000 Supreme 
Court rulings with sentences categorized by their legal 
purpose. Better extractive summarization is made possible by 
this methodical approach, which also serves as the foundation 
for weakly supervised models. 
In order to help abstractive summarizers, Elaraby and Litman 
(2022) proposed ArgLegalSumm, an argument -aware 
summarization model that incorporates sentence -level 
rhetorical roles into input embeddings. They discovered that in 
both automated and human evalu ations, summaries that 
matched argument structures performed noticeably better 
than standard models. In order to give argumentative content 
priority during the summarization and legal suggestion phases, 
CodeBandhu modifies the principle. 
2.4 Legal Solution Suggestion and Advisory Systems 
The majority of legal NLP systems currently in use end with 
classification or summarization. Work on developing advisory 
systems that produce contextual, user-friendly solutions based 
on legal documents is scarce. Platforms for legal assistance in 
India, s uch as IndiaCode or eCourts, offer users unfiltered 
information without any summarization, simplification, or 
action recommendations. 
CodeBandhu sets itself apart by putting in place a module 
that recommends solutions. This module links user inquiries 
and rhetorical roles to possible courses of action, such as 
submitting an RTI, requesting legal assistance, or looking into a 
welfare prog ram. Although rule -based chatbots are used in 
other fields, our method uses natural language processing 
(NLP) to provide intent -aware legal advice, which is a new 
development in the Indian legal technology sector. 
2.5 Evaluation of Legal NLP Systems 
Surface-level metrics like ROUGE or BLEU, which frequently 
miss legal intent or usefulness, are insufficient for evaluating 
legal summarization. An intent-based F1 evaluation metric was 
developed by Mullick et al. (2022) to address this issue. It 
verifies that the summary includes intent-relevant phrases that 
are in line with legal categories like corruption, murder, and 
land disputes. 
They also showed that their metric correlates more closely 
with human satisfaction than traditional scores, and they 
produced a manually annotated dataset of legal documents 
with tagged intent phrases. Along with ROUGE and standard 
readability (Flesch -Kincaid) scores, CodeBandhu includes this 
Intent-F1 metric in its evaluation framework. 
III. METHODOLOGY 
The architecture, elements, and processing pipeline of 
CodeBandhu—an AI -based framework for deciphering, 
summarizing, and simplifying Indian legal and government 
documents—are presented in this section. From raw 
document input to comprehensible output with  advisory 
recommendations, the system is built to offer end -to-end 
support. A lightweight recommendation engine, rhetorical role 
labeling, hybrid summarization, and unsupervised text 
simplification are just a few of the Natural Language Processing 
(NLP) components that CodeBandhu incorporates. 
3.1 System Overview: 
CodeBandhu is structured as a modular, five -stage NLP 
pipeline: 
1) Data Ingestion & Preprocessing 
2) Text Simplification 
3) Summarization Engine 
4) Rhetorical Role & Argument Mining 
5) Legal Advisory Generation 
3.2 Gathering and Preparing Data Legal documents are 
ingested by the system from various sources: 
Websites run by the government (IndiaCode, PRS India, RTI 
portals, Gazette of India) PDFs uploaded by users (court orders, 
notices, policies) Preprocessing Steps: Tesseract-OCR

is used to convert scanned PDFs into editable text through 
optical character recognition (OCR). Boilerplate material 
(headers, footers, and clause numbers) is eliminated through 
text cleaning. Sentence segmentation: Manages lengthy 
compound sentences that are frequently found in legal 
documents. Lemmatization and Tokenization: Using SpaCy to 
ensure clean input at the token level. Named Entity 
Recognition (NER): identifies legal entities like authorities, acts, 
sections, and schemes. Language Normalization: Using a 
carefully chosen lexicon, it transforms outdated terms (like 
”hereinbefore”) into their contemporary equivalents. 
3.3 Module for Text Simplification 
CodeBandhu uses a two -step unsupervised method 
modeled after USLT to simplify legal language in order to 
improve accessibility. 
Important Elements: The Legal -BERT model was refined for 
Indian legal terminology. Lexical simplification uses 
substitution lists and masked token prediction to replace 
complicated legal terms. Syntactic Simplification: Uses POS 
tagging and dependency trees to split sentences recursively. 
Enhancement of Readability: The Flesch -Kincaid readability 
score is used to assess simplified output. 
3.4 Module for Summarization 
To manage the verbosity of Indian legal documents, the 
summarization component employs a hybrid architecture. 
3.4.1 Descriptive Prior to Summarization 
• Identifies core sentences that contain important 
arguments and legal facts using LexRank or TextRank. 
• Aids in reducing document length to meet transformer 
model constraints. 
3.4.2 Summarizing Abstractively 
• Makes use of BART or PEGASUS (pretrained on 
multidocument summarization). 
• Rhetorical role annotations (refer to Section 3.5) are used 
to improve summarization by giving priority to important 
content like Issue, Conclusion, or Reasoning sentences. 
Concise, readable by humans, and context-preserving output.

3.5 Labeling Rhetorical Roles and Mining Arguments 
CodeBandhu uses rhetorical segmentation techniques 
borrowed from LawSum and ArgLegalSumm to make 
summaries and recommendations more pertinent. 
Execution: Sentences are tagged as follows using a classifier 
that has been adjusted based on rhetorical -role annotated 
data: Problem, Evidence, Argument, History, and Conclusion 
Makes it possible to prioritize segments for summarization. By 
mapping legal issues (Issue) to established solutions, tagged 
output also serves as a guide for the advisory engine. 
3.6 Engine for Legal Advice 
By offering the user recommended next steps based on 
document content and rhetorical structure, this module 
strengthens CodeBandhu’s distinctiveness. 

• It functions by extracting entities, actions, and keywords 
from the Issue and Conclusion sections. 
• connects terms to a unique database of legal answers 
based on rules: 
• For instance, ”Right to Information denied” → ”File RTI 
First Appeal” 
• ”Rejected welfare plan” → Proposal: ”Contact grievance 
redressal cell” 
• ”Rent Act eviction notice” → Recommend ”Speak with a 
tenant union or legal aid cell.” 
• Connects to real -time databases for public legal aid 
resources, like Nyaya Bandhu. 
3.7 Implementation Details 
• Preprocessing - SpaCy, Tesseract-OCR, Python regex 
• Simplification - Legal-BERT, USLT-inspired custom rules 
• Summarization - LexRank, PEGASUS / BART (via 
Hugging Face Transformers) 
• Rhetorical Role Labeling - Custom BiLSTM classifier / fine-
tuned transformer 
• Recommendation Engine - Rule-based matching + 
keyword–action mapping 
 
IV. DATASETS AND EXPERIMENTAL SETUP 
4.1  Datasets 
To evaluate CodeBandhu, we employed a combination of 
publicly available Indian legal datasets and a custom -curated 
corpus of government documents. The diversity of sources 
ensures that the system is tested on real -world documents 
beyond court judgments, including acts , policies, and citizen -
facing notices. 
4.1.1  LawSum Dataset 
Description: Introduced by Parikh et al. (2021), LawSum is the 
largest annotated dataset of Indian Supreme Court 
judgments, containing 10,000+ cases. 
Annotation Scheme: Each sentence is tagged with a 
rhetorical role (Issue, Fact, Argument, Precedent, Conclusion). 
Usage in CodeBandhu: 
• Training and evaluation of the rhetorical role 
classifier. 
• Supports argument-aware summarization by 
prioritizing Issue and Conclusion sentences. 
 
4.1.2  In-abs and In-ext Datasets 
IN-Abs: A dataset of 7,130 Indian Supreme Court judgments, 
each with manually written abstractive summaries. 
IN-Ext: A smaller dataset containing 50 documents, 
annotated by legal experts with gold -standard extractive 
summaries. 
Usage in CodeBandhu: 
• Benchmarking summarization performance. 
• Extractive summaries used for training baseline 
models; abstractive summaries used for CodeBandhu 
evaluation. 
 
4.1.3  USLT Legal Corpus 
Description: A corpus of legal documents processed through 
USLT (Unsupervised Simplification of Legal Texts) methodology. 
Usage in CodeBandhu: 
• Benchmark for text simplification. 
• Supports comparison between Legal -BERT-based 
simplification and traditional methods. 
 
4.1.4  Campus Curated Corpus (Government Documents) 
Sources: 
• IndiaCode Portal – official repository of Central Acts. 
• PRS India – policies, bills, and briefs. 
• eGazette – official notifications. 
• RTI replies and citizen -facing notices (scraped and 
anonymized). 
Size: ~1,500 documents (policies, schemes, orders). 
Purpose: 
• Test CodeBandhu’s real -world usability on non -
judicial documents. 
• Ensure the advisory engine works on practical 
government-issued texts. 
 
4.2  Preprocessing Pipelines 
1. OCR Processing: Scanned PDF documents converted 
to text using Tesseract OCR. 
2. Cleaning: Removal of headers, footers, case numbers, 
and formatting artifacts. 
3. Sentence Segmentation: Using SpaCy’s dependency 
parser, with custom rules for long compound legal 
sentences. 
4. Normalization: 
• Replacement of archaic terms (e.g., 
hereinbefore, aforesaid). 
• Standardization of references (e.g., “Section 
21 of the IPC” → “Section 21, Indian Penal 
Code”). 
5. Tokenization & Lemmatization:  Ensures input 
consistency across models. 
6. NER-based Tagging: Legal entities like Acts, Sections, 
Schemes, Authorities identified for advisory mapping. 
 
Experiments were conducted using transformer -based 
baselines (BART, PEGASUS, and Legal-BERT) and the proposed 
CodeBandhu system, implemented with PyTorch and Hugging 
Face Transformers. Evaluation combined automatic metrics 
(ROUGE, BLEU, Flesch -Kincaid readability, and Intent -F1) with 
a small -scale human study involving legal practitioners and 
students. This ensures both quantitative and qualitative 
validation of CodeBandhu’s performance. 
 
V. EVALUATION METRICS AND RESULTS 
The effectiveness of CodeBandhu was evaluated through a 
combination of automatic metrics, readability measures, and 
human evaluation studies. Results were compared against 
state-of-the-art baselines, including BART, PEGASUS, and 
Legal-BERT-based simplification models. 
 
5.1  Evaluation Metrics: 
5.1.1  Summarization Quality 
ROUGE (ROUGE -1, ROUGE -2, ROUGE -L): Measures n -gram 
overlap between generated summaries and reference 
summaries. 
BLEU: Captures fluency and phrase-level accuracy. 
 
5.1.2  Readability 
Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level:  Measures the simplicity of text, 
with lower values indicating easier comprehension. 
Average Sentence Length (ASL): Tracks syntactic simplification 
effectiveness. 
 
5.1.3 Legal Intent Coverage 
Intent-F1 Score: Following Mullick et al. (2022), this evaluates 
whether summaries capture intent -relevant phrases (e.g., 
murder, land dispute, subsidy denial). This metric is more 
meaningful in the legal domain than n-gram overlap. 
 
5.1.4 Human Evaluation 
Conducted with 10 legal practitioners and 20 law students. 
Evaluated on four criteria: 
• Accuracy (Does the output preserve the meaning?) 
• Readability (Is the language simple and clear?) 
• Relevance (Does it cover the important parts?) 
• Actionability (Are the suggestions useful for citizens?) 
• Rated on a 5 -point Likert scale  (1 = poor, 5 = 
excellent).

5.4  Discussion 
The results show that CodeBandhu significantly improves: 
• Summarization accuracy  (better ROUGE/BLEU 
scores). 
• Readability (lower Flesch score, shorter sentences). 
• Relevance to legal issues (higher Intent-F1). 
• Practical utility (human evaluators rated actionability 
much higher). 
However, limitations remain: 
• Occasional over-simplification of technical clauses. 
• Advisory suggestions are non-binding and depend on 
pre-curated mappings. 
• Multilingual legal texts (e.g., Hindi, Tamil) remain 
unsupported in this version. 
 
VI. CHALLENGES AND LIMITATIONS 
While CodeBandhu shows promising results, several 
limitations remain: 
• Legal Liability:  Users may mistake outputs for 
professional legal advice. To avoid misuse, clear 
disclaimers must state that the system is an assistive 
tool, not a lawyer. 
• Dataset Coverage: Current datasets focus mostly on 
Supreme Court judgments, which may not reflect the 
diversity of government policies and regional laws. 
Broader datasets are needed. 
• Language Limitations:  At present, CodeBandhu 
works only in English. Many Indian citizens rely on 
Hindi and other regional languages, which restricts 
accessibility. 
• OCR and Formatting Issues : Scanned government 
PDFs often introduce noise and errors, reducing 
summarization accuracy. 
 
VII. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 
7.1  Conclusion 
This paper introduced CodeBandhu, an AI -powered 
framework for simplifying, summarizing, and providing non -
binding advisory support on Indian legal and government 
documents. By integrating text simplification, rhetorical role -
aware summarization, and a lega l advisory engine, 
CodeBandhu addresses a critical gap in public legal 
accessibility. 
 
Experimental evaluation demonstrated that CodeBandhu 
outperforms baseline models such as BART and PEGASUS in 
terms of summarization quality (ROUGE, BLEU), readability 
(Flesch-Kincaid), and intent coverage (Intent -F1). Human 
evaluations with practitioners a nd law students further 
confirmed that outputs were not only accurate and readable 
but also more actionable than those of existing systems. 
 
In doing so, CodeBandhu  makes a meaningful contribution 
toward democratizing access to legal knowledge, bridging the 
gap between legal institutions and ordinary citizens. 
 
7.2  FUTURE WORK 
• Multilingual Support:  Extend the system beyond 
English to include Hindi and regional Indian languages 
using multilingual transformer models (IndicBERT, 
mBERT). 
• Voice-Enabled Interaction: Integrate a speech 
interface to support users with limited literacy or 
digital skills. 
• Integration with Government Portals : Link 
CodeBandhu with platforms such as IndiaCode, Nyaya 
Bandhu, or eCourts for real -time document access 
and legal aid. 
• Explainability: Incorporate explainable AI methods to 
show which parts of the document informed 
summaries and recommendations, increasing user 
trust. 
• Scalability and Deployment:  Optimize the pipeline 
for low -resource settings, enabling deployment on 
mobile devices or through lightweight cloud -based 
APIs. 
• Expanded Advisory Module : Develop a more 
advanced recommendation engine that combines 
rule-based legal reasoning with case -precedent 
retrieval to offer richer guidance.
